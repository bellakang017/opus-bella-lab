Marketing Research ×

Arora, Chakraborty & Nishimura (2025) · Journal of Marketing, 89(2), 43–70
Discussion led by Bella Kang · MKT 397.4 · Feb 26, 2026

the big idea — in one sentence

LLMs are not replacements for researchers — they are collaborators. The AI–human hybrid outperforms either alone in both qualitative and quantitative marketing research.

two studies

What They Tested & Found

Two empirical studies demonstrate the hybrid approach across qualitative and quantitative methods.

01 Qualitative — Friendsgiving

Method: LLM-generated interview data vs. human interviews, then hybrid analysis

LLM responses scored higher on depth (+.680, p<.05) and insightfulness (+.498, p<.05)

LLM identified niche participants humans overlooked (vegans, chefs with Friendsgiving expertise)

Hybrid outperformed both human-only and LLM-only in expert assessment

02 Quantitative — Pet Food Survey

Method: 3 LLM conditions — zero-shot, few-shot, few-shot + RAG — vs. n=605 human respondents

Zero-shot: correct direction but smaller variance (p<.05), poor correlation recovery

Few-shot: improved heterogeneity + internal consistency (p<.05)

Few-shot + RAG: best results — correlation recovery .28 → .40+

Persistent issue: LLM answers ~0.7 pts more extreme on 5-pt scales

key limitations

LLMs propagate bias from training data (gender, race, cultural)

Synthetic data shows less variance than human data — homogeneity problem

Results are illustrative, not broadly generalizable — needs replication

Not appropriate for vulnerable populations, PII, or culturally sensitive contexts

Zero-shot LLM fails on domain-specific complex tasks without context

action items — what to take away

Always use few-shot + RAG — zero-shot alone is insufficient for real research

Hybrid > either alone — design workflows where human + AI have distinct roles

Validate synthetic data against human baselines before drawing conclusions

Use LLMs for pilot testing — generate synthetic data before expensive human recruitment

Domain expertise amplifies — the human's theoretical priors determine hybrid quality

discussion questions

For Our Conversation

Questions for the class to consider.

Q1

The paper tests GPT-4 on Friendsgiving and pet food. How would you expect performance to change for topics with less internet training data — say, niche medical conditions or emerging cultural phenomena?

Q2

If synthetic respondents show less variance than real humans, what does that mean for marketing decisions that depend on understanding extreme segments?

Q3

The paper frames LLMs as collaborators. Where do you draw the line between collaboration and delegation? When does the human stop being essential?

Q4

How might you extend this framework beyond marketing — to education research, where the "respondent" could be a student, and the "product" could be a learning intervention?

What happens when you apply this framework to education research — where the "respondent" is a learner, the "product" is a learning intervention, and the AI collaborator is also the subject of study?
